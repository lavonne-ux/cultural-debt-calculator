What to Consult On
Your entry point: The VP has seen their dollar figure. Your job in the consultation is to move from "here's your number" to "here's exactly where it's coming from and what we do about it." Each driver below is a distinct consulting conversation — and a distinct revenue opportunity.
Driver 1 — Incivility & Microtoxicity
—
Consultation Talking Points
Ask: "How often do your senior engineers interrupt or dismiss junior team members in code reviews?" — this surfaces the pattern without accusation
Reference: 80% of employees lose work time after a single incident — Porath, Georgetown
Connect: Every dismissed idea or eye roll is a compounding tax on output, not a one-time event
Frame: This is a leadership modeling problem, not an HR problem
Mitigation Plan
Civility norms workshop for tech leads and senior engineers — 2 sessions
Code review protocol redesign — structured, criteria-based feedback only
Anonymous incident reporting channel — low friction, non-punitive
30-day manager check-in cadence to monitor pattern recurrence
Driver 2 — Challenger Safety Deficit
—
Consultation Talking Points
Ask: "When was the last time a junior developer changed a senior architect's decision?" — silence answers the question
Reference: Google Project Aristotle — psychological safety is the #1 predictor of engineering team performance, above talent or experience
Connect: Bugs that aren't reported early cost 5–10× more to fix in production
Frame: This is your innovation pipeline — and it's currently blocked
Mitigation Plan
Psychological safety assessment — baseline measurement by team
Challenger safety training — structured dissent protocols for tech teams
"Pre-mortem" practice built into sprint planning — formally surfaces concerns before launch
Senior engineer accountability — responses to challenges tracked and reviewed quarterly
Driver 3 — Vague Feedback
—
Consultation Talking Points
Ask: "What percentage of your 1:1s end with a specific, measurable action item for the engineer?" — most VPs don't know
Reference: Vague feedback is a rework generator — engineers optimize for the wrong output every sprint cycle it goes uncorrected
Connect: Clarity is not kindness — it is operational efficiency
Frame: Your managers are conflict-averse, not feedback-incapable — that's fixable
Mitigation Plan
SBI Feedback training — Situation, Behavior, Impact framework for all engineering managers
Feedback audit — review last quarter of 1:1 notes for specificity and follow-through
Sprint retro redesign — structured prompts that require specific, behavioral observations
Manager coaching — 4-session intensive for highest-impact leads
Driver 4 — Late Feedback & Attrition
—
Consultation Talking Points
Ask: "In your last three engineering exits — were those individuals surprised by the feedback they received before they left?" — the answer is almost always yes
Reference: Replacing a senior engineer costs 150–200% of annual salary — every avoidable exit is a 6-figure event
Connect: Late feedback is not just a culture failure — it is a budget failure
Frame: The goal is not to deliver harder feedback — it is to deliver it earlier and more often
Mitigation Plan
30-day feedback cadence mandate — no engineer goes more than 30 days without a clear performance signal
Flight risk identification protocol — early warning indicators built into manager check-ins
Exit interview redesign — structured questions that trace the feedback timeline
Stay interview program — proactive retention conversations before disengagement sets in

Objection Handling
"We already have an HR team handling this."
HR manages compliance. Cultural debt is an operational performance problem — it lives in sprint velocity, code quality, and attrition rates, not in HR policy. This engagement belongs in engineering leadership, not HR.
"I'm not sure our team has an incivility problem."
That's exactly what the data showed. 76% of engineering teams experience incivility at least monthly — most leaders don't see it because it doesn't surface through formal channels. Your calculator numbers reflect what the research says happens in organizations at your inputs, not a judgment on your team.
"What's the ROI on your engagement?"
Your assessment just showed a 7-figure cultural debt cost. A full engagement runs $25,000–$40,000. Organizations that implement these interventions with fidelity see 15–25% reduction in voluntary attrition and measurable sprint velocity gains within two quarters. The math is straightforward.
"Can we just do a one-time training?"
One-time training addresses awareness, not behavior. Cultural debt is accumulated over months or years — a single session does not reverse compounding patterns. The assessment engagement is the right entry point: low investment, high clarity, clear path forward.
